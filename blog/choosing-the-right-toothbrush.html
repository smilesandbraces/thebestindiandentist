How to Choose the Right Toothbrush & Toothpaste

Jul 15, 2025 • —

Start with the bristles: soft wins

Soft bristles flex into the gumline to remove plaque without abrading enamel. Medium and hard bristles can cause gum recession and sensitivity. If you tend to scrub hard, look for a brush with a pressure sensor or a flexible neck.

Head size & handle design

  • Choose a small to medium head to access back teeth easily.
  • Non-slip, ergonomic handles improve control and reduce over-pressure.
  • A tongue scraper on the back of the head is a helpful plus.

Manual vs electric

Manual

  • Affordable, widely available
  • Great if you have excellent technique and discipline

Electric (oscillating or sonic)

  • Built-in 2-minute timers and quadrant pacing
  • Pressure sensors prevent over-brushing
  • Helpful for braces, limited dexterity or gum issues

We often recommend a quality electric brush for consistent results—especially during orthodontic treatment.

Which toothpaste should I pick?

  • Fluoride: ~1000–1500 ppm for adults; a smear for kids under 3, pea-sized for ages 3–6.
  • Sensitivity: stannous fluoride or potassium nitrate help.
  • Whitening: avoid overly abrasive pastes if you have recession or sensitivity.
  • Tartar control: useful for heavy calculus formers—ask us first.

Special cases: braces, aligners & implants

  • Braces: orthodontic brush heads + interdental brushes under the wire.
  • Aligners: brush teeth before reinserting trays; clean trays with cool water and a soft brush.
  • Implants: super-floss or soft interdental brushes around implant crowns.

When to replace your brush

Every 3 months or when bristles splay. Replace sooner after colds or flu. A worn brush removes far less plaque.

Bottom line: A soft-bristle, small-headed brush used for two minutes twice daily with fluoride toothpaste is the safest choice for most people. Tools help, but technique wins.
